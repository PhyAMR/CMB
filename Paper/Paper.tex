\begin{abstract}
We investigate the statistical properties of the cosmic microwave
background (CMB) angular correlation function at large angular scales
($\ell \leq 30$) using Planck legacy data. Three complementary
statistics are studied: the integrated squared correlation
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, the interval-averaged correlation
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, and the antipodal
correlation $C_{180}$. Later, the statistics are combined using the
Aggregated Cauchy Association Test (ACAT) to quantify the overall
significance of the statistics, mitigating the `look-elsewhere' effect.
Theoretical distributions are generated via two independent methods,
sampling from the MCMC posterior of the standard flat $\Lambda$CDM
model fitted with the CamSpec high-multipole TT likelihood, and Monte
Carlo realizations around the best-fit spectrum, allowing a direct
comparison of the two approaches. While a lack of power correlation is
observed, we find no statistically significant evidence that the
$\Lambda$CDM model fails to fit the correlation function when
combining all the measured statistics.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{cosmic microwave background, angular correlation function, large-angle anomalies, Planck, $\Lambda$CDM}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec-intro}

Since the first measurements of the CMB temperature anisotropies, the
largest angular scales have attracted particular attention because they
probe the longest wavelength perturbations accessible within the
observable universe. Early analyses of the COBE-DMR data already
suggested that the angular two-point correlation function was
unexpectedly close to zero for separations larger than approximately
$60^\circ$ \citep{Hinshaw_1996}. Although the statistical power of
COBE was limited, this feature was subsequently confirmed with much
higher precision by the first-year WMAP observations
\citep{Spergel_2003}.

\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig-power-corr} illustrates this suppression directly for
the primary CamSpec model: the observed correlation function falls
below the theoretical $\pm 1\sigma$ band for separations
$\theta \gtrsim 60^\circ$.

To quantify the apparent suppression of correlations on large angular
scales, Spergel et al.\ introduced the statistic
\[
S_{1/2}=\int_{-1}^{1/2} C(\theta)^2\,d(\cos\theta),
\]
which measures the integrated squared correlation function over angular
separations larger than $60^\circ$ \citep{Spergel_2003}. The observed
value of $S_{1/2}$ was found to lie in the extreme tail of the
$\Lambda$CDM distribution generated from Monte Carlo realizations,
establishing the lack of large-angle correlations as one of the most
persistent large-scale anomalies in the CMB.

A series of studies by Copi, Huterer, Schwarz and Starkman strengthened
this conclusion using successive WMAP data releases. In particular,
analyses of cut-sky maps demonstrated that the observed correlation
function is nearly zero for $\theta \gtrsim 60^\circ$, with
probabilities typically below $10^{-3}$ under the standard
cosmological model \citep{Copi2006Uncorrelated, Copi2009, Copi2013Lack}.
These works emphasized that the anomaly cannot be explained simply by
the low quadrupole amplitude alone, but instead reflects an unusual
covariance structure among the lowest multipoles. They also noted
connections with other large-scale anomalies, including the
quadrupole-octopole alignment and hemispherical asymmetries.

The interpretation of the anomaly, however, remains controversial.
Efstathiou, Ma and Hanson argued that estimators based directly on
masked-sky correlation functions can exaggerate the discrepancy with
$\Lambda$CDM, and showed that full-sky maximum-likelihood
reconstructions yield results more consistent with theoretical
expectations \citep{Efstathiou_2010}. Extending this argument, Pontzen
and Peiris considered a broader class of anisotropic Gaussian models and
concluded that the observed correlation function does not constitute
compelling evidence for new physics once the full space of possible
covariance structures is taken into account \citep{Pontzen2010}. These
studies highlighted the importance of estimator choice, a posteriori
statistic selection, and the definition of the theoretical ensemble used
when assessing significance.

Another line of investigation has focused on the relationship between
the lack of large-angle correlations and other low-multipole anomalies.
Sarkar et al.\ tested whether the suppression of $C(\theta)$ is
statistically connected to the well-known quadrupole-octopole alignments
and found essentially no correlation between the two effects within
$\Lambda$CDM realizations \citep{Sarkar2010Missing}. The simultaneous
occurrence of both anomalies is therefore substantially rarer than
either anomaly considered individually. Similarly, Kim and Naselsky
argued that the observed odd-parity preference at low multipoles
provides a phenomenological explanation for the deficit of large-angle
correlations, although no convincing physical origin was identified
\citep{Kim2011}.

The anomaly has also motivated the development of alternative
statistical descriptions of large-scale correlations.
Direction-dependent generalizations of the correlation function were
introduced to investigate whether the missing power is localized on the
sky rather than uniformly distributed. Zhang constructed directional
versions of both $C(\theta)$ and $S_{1/2}$, showing that a large
fraction of the full-sky signal originates from regions associated with
the quadrupole and octopole extrema \citep{Zhang2012}. Other approaches
include bipolar harmonic decompositions \citep{Joshi2009Bipolar},
generalized isotropic angular correlation functions
\citep{2023Capturing}, geometric representations of the two-point
function \citep{Froes2015New}, and all-angle statistics such as $S_1$,
proposed to reduce the dependence on the a posteriori choice of the
$60^\circ$ threshold \citep{Gruppuso2014}.

The persistence of the anomaly in increasingly precise datasets has
further motivated investigations beyond temperature anisotropies.
Analyses of Planck polarization maps have revealed a similar suppression
of large-angle correlations, with significances comparable to those
found in temperature data \citep{Chiocchetta2020Lack-of-correlation}. If
confirmed, such results would suggest that the anomaly is not solely a
consequence of temperature-specific systematics and may instead reflect
a more fundamental property of the largest observable scales.

Despite nearly two decades of study, there is still no consensus
regarding either the statistical significance or the physical
interpretation of the lack of large-angle correlations. Part of this
ambiguity arises from the existence of many partially correlated test
statistics, each emphasizing different aspects of the low-$\ell$
temperature field. A rigorous assessment therefore requires both a
careful construction of the theoretical sampling distribution and a
framework capable of combining evidence from multiple correlated
observables. To address this problem, in this study we combine the
statistics using the ACAT test in order to measure the overall
significance of the model: although a single statistic can be
constructed to show greater significance in isolation, the overall
significance is reduced once all statistics are combined, and therefore
we obtain a more robust measure of the tension with $\Lambda$CDM.

Therefore, in this work we revisit the large-angle correlation function
using Planck 2018 data, focusing on three complementary statistics: the
integrated squared correlation $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, the
interval-averaged correlation
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, and the antipodal
correlation $C_{180}$. We construct theoretical sampling distributions
using both Monte Carlo realizations around the best-fit $\Lambda$CDM
spectrum and samples drawn from the full Planck posterior, allowing us
to disentangle cosmic variance from parameter uncertainty. Finally, we
combine the individual p-values of all statistics using the Aggregated
Cauchy Association Test (ACAT) to obtain a global measure of tension
with $\Lambda$CDM.

\section{Statistics}\label{sec-stats}

The statistics studied here are designed to probe complementary aspects
of the large-angle correlation function: its integrated power, its mean
level over specific angular ranges, and its value at antipodal
separation. The choice is guided by the existing literature on
low-$\ell$ anomalies, though as noted by \citet{Efstathiou_2010}, some
statistics, including $S_{1/2}$, were identified a posteriori from the
data, which inflates their apparent significance. Studying multiple
statistics simultaneously, and combining their p-values through a global
test, is therefore essential to obtain a reliable assessment of tension
with $\Lambda$CDM.

\subsection{Angular Power Spectrum and Correlation Function}\label{sec-corr}

The CMB temperature anisotropy $\Delta T(\hat{n})/T_0$ is expanded in
spherical harmonics with coefficients $a_{\ell m}$. For a
statistically isotropic Gaussian field the two-point statistics are
fully encoded in the angular power spectrum
$C_\ell = \langle |a_{\ell m}|^2 \rangle$. We work with the
conventional scaled spectrum $D_\ell = \ell(\ell+1)C_\ell/(2\pi)$,
which is approximately flat for a scale-invariant primordial spectrum.

The angular two-point correlation function is obtained by transforming
back to real space via a Legendre expansion,
\begin{equation}\label{eq-corr}
C(\theta) = \sum_\ell \frac{2\ell+1}{4\pi} C_\ell P_\ell(\cos\theta)
= \sum_\ell \frac{2\ell+1}{2\ell(\ell+1)} D_\ell P_\ell(\cos\theta),
\end{equation}
where $P_\ell$ are Legendre polynomials. The sum runs from
$\ell = 2$ to $\ell_{\max} = 30$ throughout this work.

\subsection{Interval Statistics}\label{sec-statistics}

Three statistics are evaluated on each of the angular intervals listed
in Table~\ref{tbl-intervals}, chosen to partition the
accessible range $[0^\circ, 180^\circ]$ into physically motivated regions while
including the classic $S_{1/2}$ interval for comparison with the
literature.

\textbf{Antipodal correlation}
$C_{180} \equiv C(\theta = 180^{\circ})$ measures the correlation
between antipodal points on the sky. Because it is a pointwise
evaluation of $C(\theta)$ it is relatively insensitive to masking
effects at intermediate scales, and it provides a direct test of the
near-vanishing of large-angle correlations at the largest possible
separation.

\textbf{Interval-averaged correlation}
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ is the mean of $C(\theta)$
over the angular interval $[\theta_a, \theta_b]$, expressed in terms
of $\mu = \cos\theta$ as
\begin{equation}\label{eq-xiv}
\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b} = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b C(\theta)\,d\mu 
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  = \sum_\ell \frac{D_\ell}{2\ell(\ell+1)}\,
\frac{P_{\ell+1}(b) - P_{\ell-1}(b) - P_{\ell+1}(a) + P_{\ell-1}(a)}{b-a},
\end{equation}
where $a = \cos\theta_b$ and $b = \cos\theta_a$. Being linear in
$D_\ell$, this statistic is sensitive to shifts in the mean
correlation level within an interval and changes sign when the
correlation function crosses zero, making it complementary to
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$.

\textbf{Integrated squared correlation} $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ is
defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq-s12}
S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b} = \int_a^b C^2(\theta)\,\sin\theta\,d\theta
= \sum_{n,m} A_n A_m D_n D_m\, T_{nm},
\end{equation}
where $A_\ell = (2\ell+1)/[2\ell(\ell+1)]$ and
\[
T_{nm} = \sum_{r=0}^{\min(m,n)}
\frac{A_r A_{m-r} A_{n-r}
\left[ P_{m+n-2r+1} - P_{m+n-2r-1}\right]_a^b}
{A_{m+n-r}(2m+2n-2r+1)}
\]
are overlap integrals of Legendre polynomials over the interval
\citep{Carlitz1961}, a similar expression to the one obtained in
\citep{Copi2009}, but extended to different angles. Being quadratic in
$D_\ell$, this statistic amplifies deviations that are coherent across
multipoles and is always non-negative, so it cannot be cancelled by sign
changes of $C(\theta)$ within the interval. The classic $S_{1/2}$
statistic of \citet{Spergel_2003} corresponds to the choice
$[\theta_a, \theta_b] = [60^\circ, 180^\circ]$.

The intervals considered are listed in
Table~\ref{tbl-intervals}, together with the classic
$[60^\circ,180^\circ]$ range used to compute $S_{1/2}$. Rather than
partitioning $[0^\circ,180^\circ]$ into fixed, evenly spaced angular
bins, the boundaries were placed at the zeros of the correlation
function measured from the observed Planck multipoles
\citep{cosmoplanck_2020}. A fixed, arbitrary binning would not be
representative of the measured $C(\theta)$: it could mix angular
ranges in which the data is predominantly positive with ranges in which
it is predominantly negative, and the resulting cancellation within
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ would dilute any genuine
mismatch between the observed and predicted correlation function.
Partitioning at the observed zero-crossings instead guarantees that each
interval corresponds to a single coherent sign region of the measured
signal, so that $\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ directly
compares the predicted and observed mean level lobe by lobe, and
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ probes the predicted and observed power
within the same regions. Figure~\ref{fig-xi-xi2} shows the resulting
partition together with both the observed and the model-predicted
correlation function $\xi(\theta)$ (top panel) and its square
$\xi^2(\theta)$ (bottom panel), illustrating that the fixed intervals
track the sign changes of the data rather than those of the
$\Lambda$CDM prediction. For each interval, the horizontal segments
mark the Planck value (red) and the model median (black), and the
vertical error bars give the model's 68\% credible interval; the
classic $[60^\circ,180^\circ]$ interval used for $S_{1/2}$ in the
literature is drawn dashed to distinguish it from the four data-driven
intervals, which are drawn solid.

We note that defining the intervals from a feature of the data under
test is itself a form of a posteriori choice, of the kind discussed by
\citet{Efstathiou_2010} (Section~\ref{sec-intro}). This
affects only the two interval-based statistics,
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ and
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$; the antipodal statistic
$C_{180}$, which yields the strongest individual tension found in this
work (Tables~\ref{tbl-bestfit-camspec}
and~\ref{tbl-mcmc-camspec}), does not depend on this
choice at all. The theoretical sampling distributions of
Section~\ref{sec-ensemble} are evaluated on exactly the
same intervals, so the reported p-values remain valid tests of whether
the observed value is unusual within each interval; what they do not
capture is the additional, harder-to-calibrate look-elsewhere effect of
having selected the interval boundaries from the same dataset being
tested. We mitigate this only partly, by retaining the classic
$[60^\circ,180^\circ]$ interval for direct comparison with the
literature and by combining all eleven statistics through ACAT rather
than reporting any single interval-based result in isolation.

\section{Data}\label{sec-data}

\subsection{Planck Posterior Chains}\label{sec-planck}

We use the publicly available Planck 2018 MCMC chains from the Planck
Legacy Archive\footnote{\url{https://pla.esac.esa.int/}}
\citep{planck_legacy_archive}. The primary dataset is the CamSpec
high-multipole TT likelihood combined with the low-$\ell$ temperature
and polarization likelihoods (\texttt{base\_CamSpecHM\_TT\_lowl\_lowE}).
This provides posterior samples of the six standard $\Lambda$CDM
parameters $\{\Omega_b h^2,\,\Omega_c h^2,\,H_0,\,\tau,\,n_s,\,A_s\}$
along with a suite of derived quantities. The Plik-based TT likelihood
and two curvature-extended runs are considered in
Appendix~\ref{sec-appendix-tables}.

For each posterior sample the theoretical power spectrum $D_\ell$ is
computed with CAMB \citep{CAMB_GitHub}, and the corresponding
$C(\theta)$ and derived statistics are evaluated analytically using
Equations~\ref{eq-corr}--\ref{eq-s12}.

\subsection{Observed Planck Multipoles}\label{sec-obs}

The experimental values of each statistic are computed from the Planck
2018 Commander temperature map and associated beam and pixel window
functions, using the same multipole range $\ell \in [2, 30]$. These
observed values serve as the reference against which the theoretical
distributions are compared.

\subsection{Ensemble Construction}\label{sec-ensemble}

Two independent strategies are used to construct the theoretical
sampling distribution. The full analysis pipeline is publicly available
\href{https://github.com/PhyAMR/CMB}{here}. The exact numerical results
reported in this paper can be reproduced from the uploaded output files;
because the pipeline uses a random seed for each realization, re-running
it from scratch will reproduce the same statistical conclusions but not
bit-identical histograms.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{MCMC ensemble.} For each of the 1000 samples drawn from the
  tail of the Planck posterior chain, specifically the last 1000 rows
  after GetDist's default burn-in removal, the cosmological parameters
  are passed to CAMB to compute a theoretical spectrum
  $D_\ell^\mathrm{th}$. Each spectrum is then passed through the
  simulation pipeline described below, producing one realization of
  cosmic variance on top of the parameter-determined
  $D_\ell^\mathrm{th}$. The resulting ensemble therefore captures both
  parameter uncertainty and cosmic variance simultaneously, constituting
  a Bayesian predictive distribution for each statistic.
\item \textbf{Best-fit ensemble.} The best-fit $D_\ell^\mathrm{bf}$ is
  read from the \texttt{.minimum.theory\_cl} file of the Planck chain,
  which contains the spectrum evaluated at the maximum-posterior
  parameter point. This single spectrum is used as input to 1000
  independent pipeline realizations, each with a different random seed.
  The spread of the resulting ensemble is driven entirely by cosmic
  variance at fixed cosmological parameters, with no contribution from
  parameter uncertainty. The width of its histograms is therefore a
  lower bound on the total variance: the additional width seen in the
  MCMC histograms is attributable purely to parameter uncertainty, and
  comparing the two ensembles provides a clean decomposition of the two
  contributions.
\end{enumerate}

In both cases, the pipeline follows the methodology of
\citet{sullivan2024methodscmbmapanalysis}. For each theoretical
$C_\ell^\mathrm{th}$ spectrum, the beam transfer function $b_\ell$
and the HEALPix pixel window function $p_\ell$ are applied, a full-sky
temperature map is synthesized at $N_\mathrm{side} = 2048$ using
\texttt{hp.synfast}, and the pseudo-$C_\ell$ are recovered with
\texttt{hp.anafast}. The recovered spectrum is then deconvolved by
$b_\ell^2 p_\ell^2$ to obtain an estimate $\hat{D}_\ell$ in the same
space as the published Planck values. When a sky mask is applied, the
recovered $C_\ell$ are corrected by the sky fraction
$f_\mathrm{sky}$ before deconvolution. At $\ell_\mathrm{max} = 30$
the beam and pixel window corrections are numerically negligible, but
they are included for consistency with the Planck data reduction.

In order to reduce the look-elsewhere effect and obtain a calibrated
global significance that accounts for the correlation structure of the
statistics without requiring an explicit model of it, we use the
Aggregated Cauchy Association Test \citep[ACAT;][]{Liu02012020}. The
eleven statistics examined, five intervals each for
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ and
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, plus $C_{180}$, are not
independent: all derive from the same realization of $D_\ell$, so
their p-values are correlated by construction. Treating them as
independent and applying a simple Bonferroni correction would be overly
conservative, while reporting only the most extreme individual p-value
would be anti-conservative.

For each statistic $k \in \{1, \dots, 11\}$ the individual p-value
$p_k$ is computed as the one-sided empirical quantile of the observed
value within the simulated ensemble: $p_k = F_k$, where $F_k$ is the
fraction of realizations with a statistic value less than or equal to
the observed one. The ACAT test statistic is then
\[
T = \sum_{k=1}^{11} w_k \tan\!\left[\left(\tfrac{1}{2} - p_k\right)\pi\right],
\]
where equal weights $w_k = 1/11$ are used, reflecting no a priori
preference for any particular angular interval or statistic. Under the
null hypothesis, $T$ follows a standard Cauchy distribution regardless
of the dependence structure among the $p_k$, so the combined p-value
is obtained analytically as
\[
p_\mathrm{global} = \tfrac{1}{2} - \frac{\arctan T}{\pi}.
\]
The Cauchy combination is exact under arbitrary dependence, including
the strong correlations expected between statistics computed over
overlapping angular ranges. The global tension in units of Gaussian
standard deviations is reported as
$n_\sigma = \Phi^{-1}(1 - p_\mathrm{global}/2)$, where $\Phi^{-1}$
is the probit function.

\section{Results}\label{results}

\subsection{Best-Fit Analysis}\label{sec-bestfit}

In the best-fit analysis the theoretical sampling distribution is
generated by Monte Carlo realizations of the CMB sky drawn from the
best-fit CamSpec $\Lambda$CDM spectrum. This approach isolates cosmic
variance from parameter uncertainty.

Figure~\ref{fig-hist-bestfit-xiv-s12}
displays the distributions of the
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ (left column) and
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ (right column) statistics, for
all five angular intervals. Each panel shows the distribution over the
1000 Monte Carlo realizations (blue histogram), and the observed Planck
value (red vertical line).
Figure~\ref{fig-hist-bestfit-c180} shows the analogous
distribution for $C_{180}$.

In that set of histograms we can see that the observed values of
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ sometimes are lower and
sometimes higher than the median of the distribution, which is
consistent with the fact that
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ is a linear statistic, and
therefore it is more sensitive to shifts in the mean correlation level,
and the change of sign in the correlation function in certain intervals.

As we can see in Table~\ref{tbl-bestfit-camspec}, for
the different intervals all values of $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ are
below the median of the distribution, which is not only consistent with
the lack of large-angle correlations but also suggests that the lack is
not only present at large angles but also at intermediate and small
angles.

\subsection{MCMC Analysis}\label{sec-mcmc}

In the MCMC analysis the theoretical sampling distribution is
constructed directly from the Planck posterior, propagating full
parameter uncertainty through to the derived statistics. The 1000
posterior samples drawn from the CamSpec $\Lambda$CDM chain each yield
a distinct $D_\ell$ spectrum, whose distribution reflects both cosmic
variance and parameter degeneracies.

Figure~\ref{fig-hist-mcmc-xiv-s12} shows the resulting distributions of
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ (left column) and
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ (right column), and
Figure~\ref{fig-hist-mcmc-c180} shows the analogous distribution for
$C_{180}$. The broader width of the MCMC-derived histograms compared
to the best-fit case reflects the additional spread due to cosmological
parameter uncertainty.

We see the same trend as in the best-fit analysis, with the observed
values of $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ consistently below the median of
the distribution. The overall tension with $\Lambda$CDM is slightly
increased in the MCMC analysis, mainly due to the additional
contribution of parameter uncertainty, which broadens the theoretical
distribution and shifts the median slightly.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec-discussion}

The results for alternative likelihoods and models with free curvature
$\Omega_k$ are reported in
Appendix~\ref{sec-appendix-tables}. The dependence of
the results on the likelihood choice (CamSpec vs.\ Plik) provides a
useful cross-check, while the curvature-extended models test whether a
departure from flatness could alleviate the observed tensions.

As noted by \citep{Kim2011}, a lack of correlation is observed across
all angular scales---including large, intermediate, and small angles.
This is reflected in the fact that the observed values of
$S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ fall below the distribution's median for all
intervals. This behavior is observed across all models and likelihoods
examined, as detailed in
Appendix~\ref{sec-appendix-tables}. While the
significance of the individual statistics is not particularly high, the
consistent pattern of low $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$ values across all
intervals suggests a coherent suppression of correlations that is not
confined to a flat model, but rather extends to models with free
curvature.

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec-conclusions}

We have studied the large-angle CMB angular correlation function and its
derived statistics using Planck 2018 data and two complementary
ensembles for the theoretical sampling distributions. The statistics
examined, the quadratic $S_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, the linear
$\langle\xi\rangle_{\theta_a}^{\theta_b}$, and the antipodal
$C_{180}$, probe distinct aspects of the large-angle temperature
field.

Both ensembles provide complementary perspectives on the significance of
the large-angle anomalies. The best-fit ensemble isolates the
contribution of cosmic variance, while the MCMC ensemble captures the
full parameter uncertainty and the cosmic variance.

The large-angle suppression of the correlation function, most evident in
$C_{180}$, is reproducible across both ensembles and across all
likelihood and curvature variants tested
(Appendix~\ref{sec-appendix-tables}), but it is not
statistically significant once the eleven correlated statistics are
combined through the ACAT global test: the resulting tension with
$\Lambda$CDM is $\sim 1.1\sigma$ for the primary
\texttt{base\_CamSpecHM\_TT\_lowl\_lowE} model
(Tables~\ref{tbl-bestfit-camspec}
and~\ref{tbl-mcmc-camspec}).

We conclude that current Planck data do not provide significant evidence
against the standard cosmological model from the large-angle correlation
function.
